Complex index of refraction¶

The index of refraction $m$ of the sphere may be complex. The real part represents the usual change in the speed of light in the medium of the sphere and the imaginary part is related to the light absorption by the sphere, $$ m = m_\mathrm{re} - j \,m_\mathrm{im} $$ The factor $m_\mathrm{im}=\kappa$ is the index of absorption or the index of attenuation.

Sign Convention¶

The sign of the imaginary part of the index of refraction assumed negative (as shown above). This convention is standard for atmospheric science and follows that of van de Hulst.

Complex Refractive Index of Water¶

Let's import and plot some data from the M.S. Thesis of D. Segelstein, "The Complex Refractive Index of Water", University of Missouri--Kansas City, (1981) to get some sense the complex index of refraction. The imaginary part shows absorption peaks at 3 and 6 microns, as well as the broad peak starting at 10 microns.

The Absorption Coefficient¶

The imaginary part of the refractive index is a non-dimensional representation of light absorption. This can be seen by writing out the $$ E(z,t) = E_0 \exp(-j (k z - \omega t)) $$ where $k$ is the complex wavenumber $$ k=k_\mathrm{re}-k_\mathrm{im}={2\pi m_\mathrm{re}\over \lambda_\mathrm{vacuum}}-j {2\pi m_\mathrm{im}\over \lambda_\mathrm{vacuum}} $$ Thus $$ E(z,t) = E_0 \exp(-k_\mathrm{im}z)\exp(j (k_\mathrm{re} z - \omega t)) $$ and the corresponding irradiance $$ I(z) = {1\over2} c\epsilon |E|^2 = I_0 \exp(-2k_\mathrm{im}z) = I_0 \exp(-\mu_a z) $$ and therefore $$ \mu_a = 2k_\mathrm{im} = {4\pi m_\mathrm{im}\over \lambda_\mathrm{vacuum}} $$ Thus the imaginary part of the index of refraction is basically just the absorption coefficient measured in wavelengths.

Size Parameters¶

Size Parameter $x$¶

The sphere size relative to the wavelength is called the size parameter $x$ $$ x = 2\pi {r/\lambda} $$ where $r$ is the radius of the sphere.

Size Parameter $\rho$¶

The value $\rho$ is also sometimes used to facilitate comparisons for spheres with different indicies of refraction $$ \rho = 2x(m-1) $$ Note that when $m=1.5$ and therefore $\rho=x$.

As can be seen in the graph below, the scattering for spheres with different indicies of refraction pretty similar when plotted against $\rho$, but no so obvious when plotted against $x$

Handling spheres embedded in a medium with index of refraction different from unity¶

The short answer is that everything just scales.

Specifically, divide the index of the sphere $m$ by the index of the surrounding material to get a relative index $m'$ $$ m' ={m\over n_\mathrm{surroundings}} $$ The wavelength in the surrounding medium $\lambda'$ is also altered $$ \lambda' = {\lambda_\mathrm{vacuum}\over n_\mathrm{surroundings}} $$ Thus, the relative size parameter $x'$ becomes $$ x' = {2 \pi r \over \lambda'}= {2 \pi r n_\mathrm{surroundings}\over \lambda_\mathrm{vacuum}} $$ Scattering calculations for an embedded sphere uses $m'$ and $x'$ instead of $m$ and $x$.

If the spheres are air ($m=1$) bubbles in water ($m=4/3$), then the relative index of refraction will be about $$ m' = m/n_\mathrm{water} \approx 1.0/(4/3) = 3/4 = 0.75 $$
